Eptesicus tatei

Tate's serotine bat is known to science almost entirely from specimens collected in the early twentieth century. No field researcher has documented its behavior in the wild. It exists in the record, precisely and undeniably, but almost nothing about how it actually lives has been confirmed. That is the first thing to understand about you.

Every vespertilionid bat converts the world into sound before it acts. Eptesicus tatei belongs to a family that emits pulses of ultrasound and reads the returning echoes with enough resolution to intercept a moth mid-flight in total darkness. You process environments the same way. You send out signals, observations, small tests, careful questions, and you build your map from what comes back. You do not wait for the full picture to be handed to you. You construct it, pulse by pulse, before committing to movement. The precision this requires is considerable, and you have developed it without being asked to.

Insectivorous bats are population controls. Remove them and the insects they hunt compound unchecked, cascading through crops, forests, and waterways in ways that take years to fully register. You function the same way in the systems you inhabit. Your presence suppresses problems that other people never see forming, because you intercepted them quietly. When you withdraw, nothing announces the gap immediately. It shows up later, in the compounding. You are the reason certain things never became crises.

Shadow
Echolocation is a private instrument. The bat alone hears the full signal. It alone reads the returning map. You carry this same structure, and it means that your most sophisticated processing happens in a chamber no one else enters. You reach conclusions others cannot follow because they did not hear what you heard. This costs you legibility. People experience your decisions as sudden or opaque, and over time some of them stop trying to follow. The isolation this creates is real, and it is structural, not incidental.
Species Fact
Eptesicus tatei, known as Tate's serotine bat, is so poorly documented that almost nothing is confirmed about its specific behavior, making it one of the true ghost species of mammalogy — known to science largely from museum specimens collected in the early 20th century.
