Species Profile
American red squirrel
Tamiasciurus hudsonicus

A single American red squirrel, weighing barely half a pound, will hold and defend a territory against every other squirrel it encounters, including its own young, driving rivals off with screaming aggression until the threat is gone. This is the animal you matched with. You are small in the ways that do not matter and enormous in the ways that do. Your presence fills more space than your size suggests it should.

The red squirrel does not scatter its stores randomly the way grey squirrels do. It centralizes everything into one midden, a single defended larder built over years, sometimes over generations, organized with a precision that makes survival computable rather than lucky. You work the same way. You pull information, resources, and relationships toward a center you control, and you track what is there with an accuracy others find almost unsettling. When you commit to a project or a person, the commitment is structural, not emotional decoration. You know exactly what you have, what it cost, and what it would mean to lose it.

Red squirrels are keystone foragers. When they cut and cache conifer cones, they bury more than they retrieve, and the forgotten stores germinate into forest. The ecosystem rebuilds itself partly on the squirrel's surplus industry. The people around you benefit from the same phenomenon. Your preparation, your systems, your refusal to leave things half-finished, these produce outcomes that outlast your direct involvement. When you disengage, the infrastructure you were quietly maintaining makes itself visible by failing. You are the reason certain things run.

Shadow
The aggression that protects a midden is the same aggression that evicts offspring before winter. The red squirrel does not negotiate territory. It holds the line absolutely, and the cost of that absolutism is borne by whoever is standing on the wrong side of it. You have ended things, cut people off, withdrawn access, with a completeness that shocked them even when it did not surprise you. The line between protection and hoarding is one you cross without always noticing, and what it costs you is the people who would have stayed if the door had stayed open one day longer.
Species Fact
A single red squirrel can hoard up to 15,000 mushrooms and cones in a single season in a centralized larder called a midden, which can be used by generations of squirrels over decades and grow large enough to be mistaken for a small hillside.
