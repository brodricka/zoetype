Pristaulacus colombianus

Pristaulacus colombianus drills through solid wood to reach something no one else can find. The target is invisible, buried, completely sealed inside timber, and this wasp locates it anyway. You operate the same way. You read rooms, situations, and people at a depth that others do not register as available information.

The wasp's antennae pick up vibrations traveling through dense wood, translating faint structural signals into precise coordinates. You do something equivalent with people. You catch the tremor beneath the composed answer, the hesitation that precedes a confident statement, the detail that does not fit the story being told. This is not intuition in the soft sense. It is pattern detection running continuously, even when you are not trying. The result is that you often know what is actually happening before the room admits it.

Aulacid wasps like this one serve as regulators inside forest ecosystems, keeping wood-boring beetle populations from hollowing out timber structures that entire communities of species depend on. You play a parallel role in the systems you inhabit. When you disengage, things drift in directions that nobody else catches in time. The people around you rely on your perception to keep certain problems from compounding quietly beneath the surface. Your presence holds a kind of structural integrity that only becomes visible once it is gone.

Shadow
The wasp deposits its egg and moves on. The host remains alive and is consumed slowly over time, and the wasp has no involvement in that process once the choice is made. You carry a version of this. Your clarity about people and situations can harden into a verdict, delivered internally, acted on through distance or withdrawal, while the other person remains unaware anything has concluded. You do not always announce the decision. You simply stop investing. What this costs you is the correction that only comes from staying inside something long enough to be wrong about it.
Species Fact
Aulacid wasps like Pristaulacus colombianus use highly sensitive antennae to detect wood-boring beetle larvae hidden deep within solid wood, then drill through the timber with a remarkably long ovipositor to deposit eggs directly on the concealed host.
