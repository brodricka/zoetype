Amarinus lacustris

Amarinus lacustris lives where almost no crab can. It completed one of the rarest evolutionary transitions in its lineage, abandoning the ocean entirely for the cold freshwater lakes and streams of New Zealand's interior. That is the first thing to understand about you. You do not require the conditions that most people in your position depend on.

This crab does not specialize. It reads whatever the environment offers, detritus, carrion, living prey, and moves toward what is present rather than waiting for what is ideal. You function the same way. You can absorb a failing project, a shifting situation, a relationship that has gone strange, and find something to work with inside it. You do not freeze when the structure changes because you were never fully dependent on the structure. What other people call instability, you call Tuesday. The precise observation is this: you process information through contact, not through planning, and you are faster than people expect.

Freshwater ecosystems in New Zealand are fragile and specific, and Amarinus lacustris holds a thread in them that nothing else holds. It cycles nutrients, clears organic matter, and occupies a niche that stays empty if it leaves. The people around you rely on something similar. You are the one who handles the thing nobody else thought to handle, the residue of situations, the loose ends that would otherwise rot. When you disengage, systems around you do not collapse dramatically. They just slowly fill with what you used to clear. You are the reason certain problems stay small.

This animal eats indiscriminately when resources are present. It does not triage. It consumes what is available because availability is the only criterion that registers under pressure. In you, this surfaces as a pattern of taking on whatever is in front of you, absorbing other people's problems, other people's messes, other people's emotional debris, without stopping to ask whether it belongs to you. You become the place where unresolved things get deposited because you are willing and capable. The cost is that your own resources erode in ways that are invisible until they are not, and by then you have already taken on the next thing.

Amarinus lacustris crossed from salt water to fresh water and stayed. That crossing is not a behavior. It is written into the animal's biology now, a permanent restructuring of how it handles osmotic pressure, how its body processes the chemistry of the world around it. You have made crossings like this. Not trips, not experiments. Permanent ones, where you rebuilt your internal architecture to survive in an environment that was never designed for you. That is not resilience as a trait. That is evolution as a life strategy, and it shows.
