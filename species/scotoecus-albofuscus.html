Scotoecus albofuscus

Scotoecus albofuscus has been classified as a species since 1870, and researchers still know almost nothing about how it actually lives. It exists in the record, confirmed and named, while its inner workings remain unobserved. You operate the same way. People have catalogued you, placed you in categories, formed confident opinions about who you are, and almost none of them have gotten close enough to see the real mechanics.

This bat hunts by emitting ultrasonic pulses, reading the echo of a world invisible to every other creature in the same airspace. It does not wait for prey to reveal itself. It sends out a signal, reads what comes back, and moves. You think this way. You gather information through channels others do not notice, process it faster than you can always explain, and arrive at conclusions that look like instinct but are actually precise calculation. The problem is that this process runs internally, silently, and the people around you rarely see the work. They see the result. They call it intuition. You know it is architecture.

You function the same way in the systems you inhabit. Your presence keeps things calibrated. When you disengage, the people around you do not always notice immediately, but the drift starts. The threads you were quietly holding begin to loosen. You are the kind of person whose absence gets diagnosed long after you have already left.

Shadow
The same ultrasonic precision that makes this bat a devastating hunter also means it operates in a frequency nobody else can hear. It is perfectly adapted to a solitary method, and that method does not translate. You carry this. Your clarity is real, your processing is accurate, and it lives almost entirely inside you. When you try to share it, the signal compresses in ways that lose the texture of what you actually saw. Over time you stop trying to translate as often, and the distance between what you understand and what others know you understand becomes a permanent gap. That gap costs you the kind of closeness that requires being fully witnessed.

Scotoecus albofuscus, known as the light-winged lesser house bat, is so obscure that almost no behavioral studies exist for it, making it one of the least-documented bat species in sub-Saharan Africa despite being classified since 1870.
