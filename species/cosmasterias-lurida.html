Cosmasterias lurida

Cosmasterias lurida digests its prey outside its own body. It pushes its stomach through its mouth, wraps it around whatever it has caught, and dissolves the living creature through direct chemical contact before pulling the meal back inside. You process the world the same way. You do not wait for things to come to you in a form you can swallow. You go out to meet them, surround them, and break them down on your own terms.

This sea star hunts along the rocky subtidal shelves of Chile and Argentina, moving with hydraulic precision across surfaces that would stop most animals cold. It does not chase. It arrives, applies pressure, and waits with the patience of something that knows chemistry is on its side. You operate the same way in rooms, in relationships, in problems. You read the structure of a situation before anyone else has named it. You find the seam where resistance is lowest and you press there, steadily, until the thing opens. The specific observation is this: you are rarely the loudest presence, and you are almost always the deciding one.

Predators like Cosmasterias lurida regulate the populations they feed on, which means their absence tips the balance of entire communities. The mussels, the barnacles, the slower invertebrates that would otherwise crowd out everything else, they proliferate without a check. The people around you depend on you to hold a line they often cannot articulate. When you step back, things drift. Standards slip. Arrangements that seemed stable reveal they were only stable because you were watching them. Your presence is structural, and the systems around you are built on it whether they say so or not.

Shadow
The stomach eversion is intimate and total. The animal surrounds what it wants to consume and dissolves it alive. There is no distance in this. You do this too, in the ways you engage with people and ideas and problems. You absorb them completely, wrap yourself around them, process them through direct contact. The cost is that you can consume what you meant only to hold. People close to you sometimes realize, too late, that they have been taken apart by the thoroughness of your attention. What it costs you is the relationships that could not survive being fully understood.
