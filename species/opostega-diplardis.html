Opostega diplardis

Opostega diplardis feeds from the inside. Its larvae enter a leaf and consume the tissue between the surfaces, hollowing it out while the leaf appears, to any outside observer, completely whole. You operate the same way. Your most significant work happens in the interior of things, invisible at the surface, and the results are structural before they are ever visible.

The leaf-mining process requires precision. The larva cannot afford to breach the outer membrane, because exposure ends survival. You have developed the same discipline around your inner life. You work through problems, relationships, and decisions from within a contained perimeter, and you do not surface conclusions until they are finished. People experience you as composed and difficult to read, and both observations are accurate. The composure is real. So is the processing underneath it. What others interpret as distance is concentration.

Remove the leaf-miners from an ecosystem and the plants they inhabit lose a check on their own cellular overgrowth. The interaction, however quiet, keeps something in balance. The people around you rely on your presence to regulate the systems you move through, often without knowing it. Your attention to the interior workings of a group, a project, a relationship, catches what surface-level observers miss entirely. When you disengage, things drift in ways that take time to name. You are the reason certain structures hold.

Shadow
The larva that mines a leaf destroys it from within. The surface stays intact, but the internal tissue is gone. You can hollow out your investment in something while keeping every external signal of engagement perfectly intact. People around you do not see withdrawal coming because you have never let them see the interior in the first place. This costs you the kind of intervention that could have changed the outcome, because by the time anyone notices, you are already done.
Species Fact
Opostegidae moths are among the most poorly studied Lepidoptera families, and Opostega diplardis is so obscure that virtually no behavioral or ecological data exists beyond its taxonomic classification, making it a ghost species in entomological literature.
