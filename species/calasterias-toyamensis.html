Calasterias toyamensis

Calasterias toyamensis lives on the seafloor of Toyama Bay and has almost never been studied. Science has a name for it, a taxonomy, a place in the family tree, and almost nothing else. You are the same way. People can label you, slot you into a category, feel certain they understand the shape of you, and still have no real data. The surface is visible. The interior remains ungathered.

Like all Asteriidae sea stars, this species digests its prey by everting its stomach outward, pulling nutrients directly from a living source through sustained, patient contact. You work the same way. You do not process the world quickly or at distance. You move close, stay close, and extract what matters through prolonged attention. Where others skim, you dissolve the thing down to its core. The people who know you have experienced this as intimacy. They have also experienced it as intensity that does not let up. You are methodical in your care, and you are equally methodical in your scrutiny.

A predator with no documented ecological record still holds a position in its system. Remove it, and something in Toyama Bay reorganizes around its absence, even if no one has written the paper explaining how. You function the same way in the rooms you inhabit. Your presence exerts pressure that others feel without being able to name. When you withdraw, the balance shifts. People notice something is different before they can identify what it is. Your influence travels through the system quietly, and its weight only becomes legible when it is gone.

Shadow
Everting your stomach is not a gentle act. It is a method of consuming something from the inside out while it is still alive. You do this interpersonally. When you are engaged with a person or problem, you press inward with sustained force, and you do not stop until you have what you came for. This can feel like devotion, and it is, but it is also consuming. People who enter your orbit closely enough find themselves being understood at a depth they did not fully consent to. The cost is this: some of them leave because the digestion felt mutual, and they were the ones being dissolved.
Species Fact
Calasterias toyamensis is a sea star so obscure that virtually no ecological data exists for it, making it one of the least-documented members of its family — a ghost species hiding in plain sight on the seafloor of Toyama Bay, Japan.
