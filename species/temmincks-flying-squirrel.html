Temminck's Flying Squirrel
Petinomys setosus

Petinomys setosus does not fly. It glides, using a membrane of skin stretched between its wrists and ankles to carry itself silently from one tree to the next through the dark. The patagium only works under pressure, when the animal commits to the leap. You are the same way: your range only opens when you leave the branch.

Almost nothing about this squirrel's daily behavior has been recorded by science. It moves through dense forest at night, reading the environment through direct contact and immediate feedback rather than established maps. You work the same way. You read a room by moving through it, not by studying it from a distance. You track what shifts, what tenses, what goes quiet. That sensitivity is not incidental to how you function. It is the mechanism itself. When the environment is honest with you, your accuracy is sharp enough to seem intuitive to everyone watching.

In the forests of Southeast Asia, gliding mammals like Petinomys setosus carry seeds and redistribute the understory by moving where heavier animals cannot reach. The canopy depends on travelers willing to cross the gaps. The people around you depend on the same thing: your willingness to go between, to carry information and energy across distances others treat as impassable. When you withdraw, the connections you maintained do not find another route. They simply close. You are the route.

Shadow
The shadow of this animal is in the flight itself. When threat appears, Petinomys setosus abandons familiar ground completely and disappears into darkness, trading security for distance. You do this with people. When something feels dangerous, you do not negotiate or hold your position. You vanish. And the cost is not just to the relationship you leave. It is to your own sense of continuity, because every retreat resets the territory, and you end up perpetually starting over in places where no one knows you yet.
Species Fact
Petinomys setosus, known as the Temminck's flying squirrel, uses a membrane called a patagium stretched between its limbs to glide silently between trees in the dense forests of Southeast Asia, yet almost nothing is documented about its behavior in the wild, making it one of the least-studied gliding mammals on Earth.
