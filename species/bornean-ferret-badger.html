Bornean Ferret-Badger
Melogale everetti

Melogale everetti is known almost entirely from museum specimens collected over a century ago. Science has named it, catalogued it, and moved on, because it never made itself easy to find. You operate the same way. You exist in the record, but the full picture of you remains in the hands of people patient enough to stay.

Everett's ferret-badger hunts the highland forests of Borneo at elevations where most predators do not bother to go. It occupies a niche by going where others thin out, carving territory through altitude and persistence rather than competition. You work the same way. You climb toward complexity that discourages other people, and you find your footing there. Where others lose interest or oxygen, you settle in. The observations you make in those high, quiet places become the clearest thinking you do.

A predator this specific fills a slot in its ecosystem that nothing else slots into cleanly. Remove it, and the food web in that highland niche loses a thread with no redundancy. The people around you feel this too. When you disengage, something stops getting done, and nobody can quite name what it was until they look for it and find it gone. You are the check on things that would otherwise run unchecked.

Shadow
Melogale everetti carries scent glands capable of delivering a chemical overwhelm, a defense so total it shuts down the threat entirely. You carry a version of this. When something closes in too fast or asks for more than you offered, you release something that clears the room. It works. The threat retreats. But the people who were not threats also step back, and some of them do not return. That reflex costs you proximity, and proximity is the one thing you need and keep losing.
Species Fact
Melogale everetti is so poorly studied that almost nothing is confirmed about its behavior, diet, or reproduction — it remains one of the least-known carnivores on Earth, known largely from a handful of museum specimens collected over a century ago.
