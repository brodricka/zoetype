Funambulus sublineatus

The dusky striped squirrel was misidentified for decades. Its markings are real, its distinctions are real, but the difference between it and its closest relatives is so precise that scientists kept collapsing it into the wrong category. You know this experience from the inside. The thing that makes you specific is the thing that most people skim past.

This squirrel occupies a narrow geographic range, the Western Ghats and Sri Lanka, and it works that terrain with total commitment. It does not migrate toward easier conditions. It learns the specific trees, the specific caches, the specific routes that make survival possible in that one place. You operate the same way. The result is that when you move, you move with accuracy most people cannot replicate.

As an omnivore in a complex forest ecosystem, this squirrel threads together resources that other animals treat as unrelated, seeds, insects, fruit, moving between layers of the food web and keeping those connections active. The systems around you depend on that same threading. You are the person who sees the link between two things no one else thought to connect, and when you withdraw, that link goes unmade. Your presence is functional, not decorative in the way of personality, but structural in the way of infrastructure. The people around you count on connections they do not know you are making.

Shadow
During scarce seasons, the dusky striped squirrel turns on its own kind. It will chase off conspecifics, the animals most like itself, from food caches it has built. The alarm calls come fast and the chasing is physical. When your resources feel threatened, you do the same thing: you become territorial toward the people who most resemble you, the ones who share your competencies or your space or your carefully tended work. You protect what you built by pushing away the people who might actually understand it best. That cost is real, and it falls on exactly the relationships that could have held.
Species Fact
The dusky striped squirrel is among the smallest palm squirrels in India, restricted to the Western Ghats and Sri Lanka, and its striping pattern is so subtle it was long confused with closely related species, causing taxonomic uncertainty that persisted for decades.
